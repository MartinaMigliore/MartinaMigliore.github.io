    We've just put up the first nominations for our new Feature, "The Ten-Second Top Fifty," our list of the fifty greatest pop music soundbites of all time, sponsored by Music Boulevard.. Write in with your own candidates in the Feedbag discussion area. In an age of fragmentation, who bothers to listen through an entire song any more? Sure the mainstream music mags still roll out the obligatory "Album of Year" awards this month, but the real audiostream running through our days is more disjointed, musical notes scattered about like so many TCP/IP packets. Soundbites, samples, riffs, solos, refrains, intro chords -- blurted out in commercials, lingering in the choppy wakes of channel surfers, all rigged together into a complex musical pastiche. In the spirit of this schizophrenic soundtrack, FEED hereby presents the "Ten Second Top Fifty," in which a motley panel of critics, musicians, netheads -- and you, gentle readers -- nominate the fifty greatest pop music fragments of all time ...

    DOCUMENT

    Our panel of experts -- FEED's David Greenberg, NYU's Ellen Willis, The Weekly Standard's Matthew Rees, and MSNBC commentator Laura Ingraham -- dissects the Dole and Clinton campaign books in "Between Hype and Hypocrisy."Though little noticed at the time, April 30, 1991 was a historic day in the annals of American presidential politics. For it was on that blustery morning in Lowell, Massachusetts that former Democratic senator Paul Tsongas announced his intention to run for president. While the announcement itself was remarkable, considering George Bush's seemingly-invincible status, more noteworthy was that Tsongas simultaneously released an 85-page manifesto spelling his views on subjects ranging from capital gains taxes to abortion. However elementary this may have been, no major party candidate had ever published his views in such a detailed format. Tsongas dubbed his work A Call to Economic Arms and its high-minded tone helped establish that this awkward figure who had been out of politics for over six years was a serious candidate. ...
